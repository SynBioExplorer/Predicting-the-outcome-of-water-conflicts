# Peer Review: Predicting Transboundary Water Conflict Outcomes

**Journal**: *Global Environmental Change*

**Reviewer**: Reviewer 1

**Expertise**: Quantitative political science, environmental security, conflict prediction

**Date**: 20 March 2026

---

## 1. Summary

This paper presents a machine learning benchmark for predicting the ordinal intensity of transboundary water events using the TFDD, evaluating eight external data sources through a sequential ablation protocol. The authors report that economic indicators and temporal dynamics outperform climate variables, which are found to degrade prediction, and that the rate of treaty formation matters more than the accumulated stock of treaties. The best model, an Optuna-tuned XGBoost classifier, achieves a quadratic weighted kappa of 0.523 on a validation set, declining to 0.293 on a held-out test set covering 2003-2008.

## 2. Significance and Novelty

The paper addresses a genuine gap. Prior work on transboundary water conflict has treated the BAR scale as binary or continuous, and no published study has conducted systematic feature ablation across the range of data sources assembled here. The idea of testing which data sources actually contribute predictive power, rather than simply throwing everything into a model, is methodologically sound in principle. The temporal SHAP decomposition is a useful contribution that goes beyond static feature importance. However, the significance of the contribution is substantially undermined by methodological concerns detailed below, particularly regarding the ablation design, the validity of the temporal split, and the strength of the causal language used to interpret what is fundamentally a correlational exercise.

## 3. Major Concerns

1. **The sequential ablation protocol is path-dependent and does not constitute a valid test of feature group value.** The authors add feature groups one at a time to a growing retained set and discard any group that does not improve QWK at that step. This procedure is order-dependent: the value of climate features is evaluated against the 29-feature TFDD baseline alone, while governance is evaluated against the same baseline, but economic features are evaluated after the baseline has already been retained. Had the order been different (e.g., climate tested after economic features are already in the model), the marginal contribution could change. The authors present seven sequential tests but do not report (a) all pairwise or combinatorial comparisons, (b) any sensitivity analysis to ordering, or (c) confidence intervals on the ablation deltas. A delta of -0.053 for climate and +0.044 for economics look meaningful, but without bootstrap variance estimates on these deltas under the fixed LightGBM default-hyperparameter protocol, we cannot distinguish signal from noise. The entire ablation result, and by extension the paper's headline claim, rests on point estimates from a single train/validation split with default hyperparameters. This is insufficient evidence for the strong conclusion that "climate variables provide no predictive gain."

2. **The temporal split introduces severe distributional artifacts, and the validation-to-test degradation is not adequately addressed.** The training set (pre-1996), validation set (1996-2002), and test set (2003-2008) each span different geopolitical regimes. The authors acknowledge this but frame it as "informative rather than disqualifying." I disagree with this characterization. A QWK drop from 0.523 to 0.293 represents a 44% decline, which indicates the model is substantially overfitting to the distributional properties of the validation era. More critically, the class distribution likely shifts across these periods: the post-2000 era saw different patterns of cooperation and conflict than the Cold War training period. The authors do not report the class distribution across their three temporal splits. If, for example, the proportion of conflict events increases in the test period (they note post-1975 events are marginally more conflictual), then a model tuned to the validation era's class balance will systematically misclassify. The ablation itself was conducted on the validation split, meaning the feature selection is optimized for 1996-2002 dynamics; the feature groups that are "best" for predicting 1996-2002 outcomes may not be the groups best suited for 2003-2008 or for future forecasting. The authors must at minimum report class distributions per split and ideally test ablation stability using rolling temporal windows.

3. **Endogeneity of temporal features renders the cooperation momentum and events_prior_5yr features problematic.** Cooperation momentum is defined as the rolling mean BAR of prior events in the same basin. This feature is mechanically correlated with the target: basins that have historically cooperative events will have high cooperation momentum and are likely to have cooperative future events, not because momentum causes cooperation but because unobserved basin-level confounders (institutional quality, economic interdependence, cultural affinity) drive both. The same applies to events_prior_5yr, which captures basin activity intensity. Including these features inflates apparent model performance while obscuring the true causal structure. The SHAP importance attributed to these features (ranks 2 and 3 globally) may reflect this circularity rather than genuine predictive mechanisms. The authors should (a) report model performance with and without these autoregressive features to quantify the inflation, and (b) discuss the endogeneity concern explicitly rather than interpreting these features as evidence of "strong path dependence in conflict dynamics."

4. **The claim that "climate doesn't matter" is overreaching given the spatial and temporal scale mismatch.** The authors use basin-averaged annual climate variables (precipitation, PET, SPEI-3) to predict event-level outcomes. As they briefly acknowledge, this averaging likely smooths out the localized, short-duration hydrological shocks that matter. But the logical implication of this acknowledgment directly undermines the headline finding: the absence of predictive power from poorly specified climate features does not demonstrate that climate is irrelevant; it demonstrates that the climate features tested at this resolution are uninformative. This is a measurement problem, not a substantive finding about climate-conflict linkages. The title ("Economic and Temporal Drivers Outweigh Climate Signals") and the abstract ("climate variables provide no predictive gain") substantially overstate what the evidence supports. At minimum, the authors should reframe these claims to specify the resolution and feature specification under which climate signals were tested, and they should discuss what finer-grained climate features (e.g., sub-annual anomalies, flow-specific indicators, upstream precipitation shocks) might perform differently.

5. **QWK may not be the most appropriate primary metric for this problem, and the macro-F1 results tell a different story.** QWK is designed for inter-rater agreement and penalizes distant misclassifications quadratically. While it respects ordinality, it is dominated by the majority class. With mild cooperation comprising 51.9% of events and neutral only 4.0%, QWK will primarily reflect the model's ability to classify the majority class correctly. The Optuna-tuned models actually show *lower* macro-F1 (0.375-0.383) than the default models (0.388-0.418), meaning that hyperparameter tuning improved QWK at the expense of per-class balance. This is a significant concern: the tuned model is better at predicting the majority class but worse at distinguishing conflict from cooperation, which is presumably the task's primary purpose. The authors should report per-class precision, recall, and F1 for all models (they reference Extended Data Table 2 but do not discuss it), and should justify why QWK is preferred over metrics that weight minority-class performance more heavily, such as macro-F1 or class-weighted QWK.

## 4. Minor Concerns

1. **Missing key citations.** The paper does not cite Bernauer and Siegfried (2012, *J. Peace Res.*), who conducted an early quantitative analysis of climate variability and water cooperation/conflict, or Brochmann and Hensel (2009, *J. Peace Res.*), who examined institutional management of river claims. The discussion of hydro-hegemony would benefit from citing Warner and Zawahri (2012). The reference to "autoresearch methodology (Karpathy, 2024)" appears to be a blog post or informal publication and is not appropriate as a methodological citation in a peer-reviewed journal. The authors should cite the underlying methodological principles (e.g., sequential feature selection, wrapper methods) from the machine learning literature.

2. **The 4-class ordinal grouping is not well justified.** The authors state that the grouping "preserves ordinality while providing sufficient class sizes" but do not discuss why BAR = 0 (neutral) is treated as its own class despite comprising only 4.0% of events. This extreme class imbalance will cause systematic misclassification of neutral events and inflate QWK. The authors acknowledge that "alternative groupings may yield different results" but do not test any. At least one sensitivity analysis with a 3-class grouping (merging neutral with mild cooperation) should be reported.

3. **The SHAP temporal decomposition lacks statistical inference.** Reporting that treaty formation rate "increased 56.5% in importance" across eras is a point estimate computed over different-sized subsets of data. There is no test of whether this change is statistically significant, no confidence interval, and no control for the changing composition of basins active in each era. The Cold War era has far more events than the post-2000 era, meaning the SHAP estimates have different precision, and shifts in basin composition (e.g., more Mekong events post-1995) could drive apparent importance changes without any genuine shift in the underlying mechanism.

4. **The "year" feature is problematic.** Year of occurrence ranks 4th in SHAP importance. This feature encodes secular trends and is a proxy for all time-varying confounders. Its high importance likely reflects the model using year as a catch-all for unmodeled temporal dynamics rather than capturing a meaningful predictor. Including raw year as a feature is a well-known anti-pattern in temporal prediction because it guarantees that the model cannot extrapolate beyond the training period. This likely contributes to the validation-to-test degradation.

5. **Reproducibility concerns.** The paper references Optuna hyperparameter tuning with 100 trials but does not report the search space, the best hyperparameter values, or the sensitivity of performance to hyperparameter choices (Extended Data Figure 2 is referenced but not shown). The code and data should be made publicly available; there is no data availability statement or code repository link in the manuscript.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Have you tested the ablation protocol under different orderings of feature groups? Specifically, what is the marginal value of climate features when added to the model that already contains economic and temporal features (the final 45-feature model), rather than to the 29-feature baseline? If climate still degrades performance in this context, the finding is more robust; if it does not, the sequential protocol has produced a misleading result.

2. What is the model's performance when cooperation momentum and events_prior_5yr are excluded? These autoregressive features are the most likely sources of endogeneity-driven performance inflation, and understanding the model's predictive skill without them is essential for interpreting the contribution of exogenous predictors like economic indicators and treaty formation rates.

3. The TFDD encodes events at heterogeneous levels of granularity, from formal treaty signings to minor diplomatic exchanges. Have you examined whether model performance varies by event type or source? If the model primarily succeeds on well-documented formal events and fails on informal interactions, the practical utility for early warning would be substantially limited.

## 6. Recommendation

**Major Revision.**

The paper addresses an important question and assembles a valuable dataset, but the methodological concerns are substantial. The path-dependent ablation protocol, the unexamined endogeneity of autoregressive features, the overstatement of the climate finding, the troubling QWK-to-macro-F1 tradeoff, and the unexplained 44% performance degradation on the test set all require significant additional analysis before the paper's conclusions can be considered reliable. I would welcome a revised version that (a) replaces or supplements the sequential ablation with a combinatorial or permutation-based feature importance approach, (b) reports class distributions per split and ablation stability across rolling windows, (c) quantifies the performance contribution of autoregressive features separately, (d) reframes the climate finding to reflect the resolution limitations of the climate features tested, and (e) discusses the QWK vs. macro-F1 tradeoff and its implications for minority-class prediction. With these revisions, the paper could make a meaningful contribution to the quantitative conflict prediction literature.
